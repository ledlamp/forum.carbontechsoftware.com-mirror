I call this missing the target and hitting someone's crotch instead. Let me re-state what I was trying to say about upgrades:


The reason I don't like upgrades and don't like the idea behind them is that they upgrade a weapon so it is absolutely better than the previous version of the weapon. This would be fine and dandy in a single player game facing CPU opponents, but this is a multiplayer game facing humans. So every advantage you give to one player makes the other player's experience harder for them. We can still have a store and equipment, but they need to be balanced so that while one player may like one weapon better and perform better with it, another player may find it to be less effective. The best example of multiplayer like this is console shooters; COD is the shining example of a (mostly) balanced system of equipment swaps as well as BF, and while Halo still goes by old school pickups you need to play to a weapon or piece of equipment's strengths to use it effectively (for instance a tank will beat a standard armed soldier most days, but give that soldier an EMP or Anti-Vehicle weapon and they blow up the tank, though they are now more vulnerable to standard soldiers armed with anti-personal weapons). There still is a sense of upgrades in these games that crave, but it doesn't harm other players' experiences. Sure if you need to earn these upgrades less people have them, but it is also harder for people to get leaving those who have them in a much better position.


Alternatively, we can up the power/starting upgrade level of the starting weapons or give players more starting credits to upgrade their equipment. That way (assuming we fix all the OP weapons) new players stand at least stand a chance against other more well-armed players, so the game is less about how long you have been playing and more about how skilled you are (AKA instead of saying "if I had a shotgun I would have killed you" you say "damn, that guy has skills").
